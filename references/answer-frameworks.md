# Answer frameworks (per-question structures)

Proven, question-specific structures for the most common interview questions. When a
question here matches (or closely matches) the one you're writing, **use this structure** for
its AI answer instead of the generic type-recipe in SKILL.md step 7. These beat generic
recipes because they're battle-tested shapes interviewers respond to.

Always fill the structure with the **candidate's real history** and the **target role/company**
— the framework is the skeleton, their story is the flesh. Match the interview language.

> The examples below use a **fictional candidate ("Alex")** with a mobile-games/product
> background purely to illustrate the shape. Replace with the real candidate's history.

This file grows: add new frameworks as the user supplies them.

---

## "Tell me about yourself" / 请做个自我介绍

A 4-part structure (keep it ~90 seconds to 2 minutes):

1. **Snapshot of your work history** — a quick, coherent arc of where you've been (not a
   chronological CV read-out; the throughline that leads to *this* role).
2. **Achievement-oriented mini-stories** — 1–2 concrete wins with numbers, not just duties.
   Pick the achievements most relevant to this role.
3. **What you KNOW about this role** — show you understand what the job actually is. This
   signals you've done your homework and pivots the answer toward them.
4. **WHY you're the RIGHT fit** — connect your background directly to what *they* need.
   End on the match, not on yourself.

**Example (Alex → Lumina China PM, English):**

> *(1 · snapshot)* I've spent the last six years building engaging mobile products, mostly in
> the Chinese market — I started in marketing at BrightWave, then moved into games as a
> Publishing Manager at NovaPlay and a Producer at PixelForge.
>
> *(2 · achievements)* Along the way I've shipped at real scale: I took Doodle Keyboard from
> concept to 50M+ downloads, and built Idle Rescue to 11M+ while lifting its retention
> about 7% through data-driven iteration.
>
> *(3 · what I know about the role)* I know this role is about owning content and monetization
> products end to end, bridging Lumina's China teams with the global org and localizing for
> the market.
>
> *(4 · why I fit)* That's exactly my intersection — product ownership, data-driven iteration,
> deep China-market knowledge, and I'm fully bilingual, so I can turn China's product
> strengths into global wins.

Note how each tip maps to one short paragraph. Keep tip 4 tight — it's the landing.

## "Why are you the right fit for this role?" / 为什么你适合这个岗位？

Some tips shape the **content** of the answer; two shape the **delivery** — split them: content
tips go in the AI answer, delivery tips go in the Strategy field as live coaching.

*Content (→ AI answer):*
1. **Understand their PAIN and how to solve it** — open by naming the real problem this role
   exists to solve, then position yourself as the solution. Lead with *their* need, not your CV.
2. **Tell stories** — back the claim with a concrete story (with numbers), not adjectives.
3. **Sell yourself** — close with a clear, confident value statement: the specific combination
   they get by hiring you.

*Delivery (→ Strategy field):*
4. **Show the right demeanour** — calm, warm, engaged; you're not pleading, you're the obvious
   answer to their problem.
5. **Be confidently YOU** — let your genuine self show; conviction beats a polished script.

**Example (Alex → Lumina China PM, English):**

> *(1 · their pain + how I solve it)* The hard part of this role is turning China's product and
> creator strengths into products that win globally — and that takes someone who genuinely
> lives on both sides. That's the gap I fill.
>
> *(2 · story)* At PixelForge I was the bridge between our Chinese team and overseas partners like
> Skyline Games — I translated market insight into product and shipped Idle Rescue to 11M+,
> lifting retention ~7% on data; at NovaPlay I took Doodle Keyboard to 50M+.
>
> *(3 · sell yourself)* So you'd get a product owner who ships at scale, decides on data, knows
> the China market cold, and is fully bilingual — the exact combination this role is built for.

**Strategy field for this question:** "Deliver with calm confidence — you're not asking for
the job, you're showing you're the obvious answer to their problem. Lead with THEIR pain, back
it with one real story, close with a crisp value statement. Be warm, direct, and genuinely
yourself — conviction lands harder than a polished script."

## "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" / 五年后你希望做到什么？

A 3-part structure:

1. **Focus on WHAT you'll be doing** — talk about the *work and impact*, not a job title.
   Never say "I want your job" or name a specific rank; describe the kind of problems you
   want to be owning.
2. **Skills you want to develop within 5 years** — concrete capabilities you'll grow, ideally
   ones this role/company uniquely lets you build.
3. **Signal you can stay long-term** — reassure the interviewer you're not a flight risk;
   frame this as a place you'd want to grow into and build something lasting.

**Example (Alex → Lumina China PM, English):**

> *(1 · what you'll be doing)* In five years I want to be deeply owning content and monetization
> products at global scale — shaping how China's product and creator strengths translate into
> products that win across markets.
>
> *(2 · skills to develop)* Along the way I want to sharpen skills I can only build here: large-
> scale content and ads product, data at Lumina's scale, and cross-market product strategy —
> going deeper than mobile games let me.
>
> *(3 · long-term signal)* Honestly this is somewhere I'd want to grow into for the long run —
> Lumina is where those skills compound, and I'd rather go deep and build something lasting
> than keep switching.

**Strategy field for this question:** "Talk about the work, never a title — don't say you want
the interviewer's job. Name concrete skills this role uniquely builds, and close by signaling
you're here to stay and grow, not a flight risk. Don't mention founding a company or leaving."

## ALL behavioral questions ("tell me about a time…") — CARL

Use this for **every behavioral / "tell me about a time…" question** (conflict, failure,
challenge, leadership, influence, ambiguity, mistake, etc.). Four parts, short prose:

1. **Context** — what was happening? (set the scene in one line)
2. **Action** — what did *I personally* do? (this is the heart — "I", not "we")
3. **Result** — what measurable outcome followed? (numbers where possible)
4. **Learning** — what did you take away or change afterward? (always end here)

**Example (Alex, "Tell me about a challenge you faced," English):**

> *(Context)* At NovaPlay I was working with a studio that had shipped 60+ games without a
> single hit; they pitched Doodle Keyboard but resisted it because the liquid and shader effects
> were technically hard, and then wanted to test a version I didn't think was ready.
>
> *(Action)* Rather than pull rank, I made the product case with concrete references, reframed
> the technical difficulty as the market differentiator, and held the quality gate until the
> build was strong enough to test honestly.
>
> *(Result)* It tested at a strong CPI and grew to 50M+ downloads, and the studio became a
> strategic partner.
>
> *(Learning)* It taught me that disagreement is won with evidence and a testable next step,
> not authority — a habit I use on every studio call now.

**Strategy field for behavioral questions:** "Use Context → Action → Result → Learning. Spend
most of it on YOUR Action ('I', not 'we'), give a measurable Result, and always end on the
Learning. Pick a story that shows the competency this question is really testing."

## "What is your biggest weakness?" / 你最大的弱点是什么？

A fill-in-the-blank growth arc. The key move: describe the **behaviour that exhibits** the
weakness (not just an abstract label), then self-awareness → fix → positive result. Pick a
real, non-disqualifying weakness (never one core to the job). Use these sentence starters:

1. "In situations where ___" *(the context/situation)*
2. "I have tended to ___" *(the WAY you exhibit the weakness — the behaviour, not the label)*
3. "This has made me appear ___" *(name the weakness here)*
4. "So I've learned to be cognizant of this and no longer ___" *(the old behaviour)*
5. "Instead, I now ___" *(your fix)*
6. "This has resulted in ___" *(the successful result)*

**Example (Alex, English):**

> *(1)* In situations where I strongly believed a product decision was right, *(2)* I tended to
> focus on proving the judgment on its merits and pushing it through. *(3)* This has made me
> appear too focused on being right, and not attentive enough to building alignment. *(4)* So
> I've learned to be cognizant of this and no longer lead with just the conclusion. *(5)*
> Instead, I now front-load the decision framework, surface objections early, and build shared
> ownership before execution. *(6)* This has resulted in far smoother buy-in — my recent product
> calls landed with the team behind them, not just approved.

**Strategy field for this question:** "Pick a real, non-disqualifying weakness (never one core
to the job). Describe the *behaviour* that shows it, not an abstract label, then walk the arc:
self-awareness → the fix → a concrete better result. Avoid humble-brags like 'I work too hard.'"

## "Why did you leave / why are you leaving?" / 为什么离开？

Pick the variant matching the candidate's real situation. The universal shape is the same:
**(1) state the reason briefly and neutrally — never bitter, never badmouth → (2) distance it
from your capability / reassure → (3) pivot forward to why you fit THIS role.** Keep it short;
don't dwell.

**Situation 1 — mass layoff / restructuring (external, not you):**

> Unfortunately the company lost a major client, so cost-cutting measures were implemented and
> some positions were eliminated. I was among the most recently hired in our department, so I
> was part of the group let go. I do have strong performance reviews, and my former manager
> will happily provide a good reference. And because my previous position was very similar to
> this role, I'm confident I can transfer what I gained here quickly.

*(reason → not-performance + reference → bridge to fit)*

**Situation 2 — role/fit or performance issue (framed gracefully):**

> After some management changes, it became clear the new director had different expectations
> for my role that didn't line up with my strengths, and he eventually brought in someone with
> more sales experience. The experience actually clarified where my real strength is —
> financial modelling — and I know I'd be a major asset in a role like this one, which focuses
> on budgets and forecasts. I'd be happy to walk you through that expertise.

*(neutral external framing → reframe as learning about your real strength → bridge to fit;
never attack the manager)*

**Situation 3 — voluntary, seeking better growth (written model):**

> I've genuinely valued my time there and I'm proud of what I built — [one concrete
> achievement]. But I've grown about as far as this role allows, and I'm looking for [bigger
> scope / a new challenge / to build X]. I'd rather make a deliberate move toward the right next
> step than stay comfortable, and this role is exactly that step — which is why I'm here.

*(gratitude + real achievement → "growth," not "escape" → connect to what THIS role uniquely
offers; never frame it as running away from something)*

**Strategy field for this question:** "Keep it brief and neutral — never bitter, never badmouth
a manager or company. State the reason, distance it from your capability (reference, strong
reviews, or it was structural), then pivot fast to why you fit THIS role. For a layoff, make
clear it was structural (last-in-first-out), not performance. For voluntary moves, frame it as
growth toward something, not escape from something."

## "Tell me about a time you failed" / 讲一次你失败的经历

A behavioral question (use the CARL spine) with three failure-specific moves on top:

1. **Define failure** — open by framing what *you* count as failure. This puts you in control
   of the story and shows maturity (e.g. "I count it as failure when the execution worked but
   I still didn't protect the outcome").
2. **Focus on ONE thing** — a single, real, substantial failure. Don't hedge with several
   small ones or a fake "failure." Own it clearly.
3. **End with a learning** — the point of the whole answer. What you understood and *changed*.
   This is mandatory here (not optional as in a generic behavioral question).

**Example (Alex, English):**

> *(Define)* I'd define my biggest failure as a time I built all the right operational pieces
> but still let the product outcome slip.
>
> *(One thing · CARL)* At PixelForge I owned our Skyline Games partnership end to end — I built the art
> pipeline, negotiated better terms, and delivered four prototypes. But none hit their targets.
> The root cause was mine: the pressure to keep shipping quietly eroded our concept-selection
> bar, and I raised the capacity problem too late.
>
> *(Learning)* What I changed: I now protect the greenlight standard even at the cost of fewer
> launches — execution can't rescue a weak product hypothesis — and I separate ideation from
> delivery and set the kill line upfront.

**Strategy field for this question:** "Open by defining what you count as failure — it puts you
in control. Pick ONE real, substantial failure (never a fake or trivial one), own the root
cause as yours, and spend the ending on what you learned and *changed*. Use CARL for the middle.
The learning is the whole point — don't skip it."

## "What is your greatest strength?" / 你最大的优势是什么？

The one rule: **pick a strength with DIRECT application to this job.** Everything else follows.

1. **Choose a strength that maps to the role's real needs** — read the JD and pick the strength
   it most needs. NOT a generic technical skill, NOT an irrelevant strength you happen to have.
2. **Back it with a short story** — show the strength in action (CARL-lite), don't just assert
   it. Include a **learning** as part of the story, so it reads as earned, not boastful.
3. **Connect it explicitly to this role** — say how that strength will move the needle here.

**Example (Alex → product role, English):**

> *(1 · strength w/ direct application)* My biggest strength is turning a market insight into a
> product that ships and performs — and validating it on data, not opinion. That's exactly what
> this role needs.
>
> *(2 · story + learning)* At PixelForge I spotted a familiar user behaviour, turned it into Idle
> Rescue, and when retention plateaued I didn't guess — I read the drop-off curve, formed a
> hypothesis, and tested dialogue choices, lifting retention ~7%. The learning that stuck: the
> strength isn't having the idea, it's validating it cheaply before betting big.
>
> *(3 · connect to role)* At this role's scale, that instinct — insight → shipped product →
> data-driven iteration — is what actually moves the metrics.

**Strategy field for this question:** "Pick a strength with DIRECT application to the JD — never
a random technical skill or an irrelevant one. Back it with a short story that shows it in
action and includes a learning, so it sounds earned, not boastful. Close by tying it to how it
helps in THIS role."
